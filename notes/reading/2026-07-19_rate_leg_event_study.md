# 2026-07-19 — The loss→rate event study and the scenario fallback (read-log)

> Session: `OQ-INT-09` closed (`INT-18`, `MKT-SIE-06/07`) — SIE Python client + full download, Bonos M yields from dirty prices, pre-registered event study of MXN sovereign pillars around 94 CENAPRED episodes → **null** (Mexican yields bound the per-event response near zero); per the blind-fixed gate the rate channel carries the `[Anyfantaki2025]` literature scenario ($S_{\text{rate,eff}} = 196{,}734{,}670$ MDP-2025, sign $+1$).

1. **MacKinlay — *Event Studies in Economics and Finance*, JEL 35(1), 13–39** (`[MacKinlay1997]`, §99, new): the market-model event-study design — estimation window, abnormal returns $AR_t = \Delta y_t - (\hat a + \hat b\,\Delta y^{US}_t)$, CAR aggregation, and its inference limits. **Why:** `INT-18`'s spec (windows $[-120,-10]$ / $[0,+5]$ BD, market model on matched US Treasury controls, cross-sectional CAR-on-loss regression) is this design transplanted to yields; without it the window and control choices look arbitrary.
2. **Anyfantaki, Blix Grimaldi, Madeira, Malovaná & Papadopoulos — *Decoding climate-related risks in sovereign bond pricing*, ECB WP 3135, esp. eq. (2) (p. 25) and Figure 8** (`[Anyfantaki2025]`, new, verified): lag-augmented local projections of log 10Y local-currency yields on EM-DAT climate-disaster damage (% of GDP), EMDE panel. **Why:** it is the source of the fallback $\beta = 4\times10^{-6}$ per bn MDP-2025 now wired in `configs/climate_jump_real.yaml`; the read must confirm the regressor-unit interpretation and the IRF-horizon caveat (peak at years 2–5, ≈0 in year 0–1 — injected as an immediate jump it is an upper bound) documented in `configs/loss_to_rate_scale.yaml`.
3. **Andersen & Piterbarg — *Interest Rate Modeling*, the affine HW1F bond-pricing sections around Prop. 10.1.7** (`[AndersenPiterbarg2010]`): the zero-coupon yield loading of a short-rate factor, $B(T)/T = (1 - e^{-\alpha T})/(\alpha T)$. **Why:** `rate_scale_from_beta` inverts exactly this loading to convert an observed pillar-yield slope $\beta_T$ into an equivalent short-rate jump ($J = \beta_T\,\alpha T/(1-e^{-\alpha T})$); it is also why the conversion deliberately uses the *engine's* $\alpha$ (the jump must reproduce $\beta_T$ inside the engine's own dynamics).
4. **Klusak, Agarwala, Burke, Kraemer & Mohaddes — *Rising Temperatures, Falling Ratings*, Management Science 69(12), §6 + Appendix D** (`[Klusak2023]`, now verified in §10): Mexico −5.55 notches under RCP 8.5 by 2100, ratings→spread 8–12 bp per notch. **Why:** the century-scenario anchor that frames what the per-event null does *not* contradict — slow climate repricing of the sovereign can coexist with no detectable per-event reaction; feeds the manuscript discussion of the long-horizon rate leg.
5. **Klomp — *Do natural disasters affect monetary policy?*, J. Macroeconomics 64, 103164** (`[Klomp2020]`, verified, direction only): flexible central banks *ease* after disasters. **Why:** the sign caveat behind reading the short-end pillars (TIIE 28) — a policy easing can offset a credit-premium rise, one reason the short end is the least informative pillar and the gate targets the long end.
6. **Cevik & Jalles — *This changes everything*, Energy Economics 107** (`[CevikJalles2022]`) **and Mallucci — *Natural disasters, climate change, and sovereign risk*, JIE 139** (`[Mallucci2022]`): spreads price climate *vulnerability* (index-based; Mexico in sample) and small-island hurricane-*risk* premia (74–126 bp). **Why:** external-validity brackets for `INT-18` — what the literature actually measures (vulnerability levels, regime premia) vs what the thesis needed (a per-event damage elasticity), and why the ECB panel was the only unit-compatible source.

Open decisions these readings feed: the `OQ-HAZ-18` extended-window re-run (Otis 2023 — the strongest candidate for breaking the null), the Mexican-$\alpha$ recompute and calibration (`OQ-MKT-12`), and the headline-metric choice (`OQ-INT-02`).
